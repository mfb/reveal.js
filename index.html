Day-to-day basic security:

Screen lock your phone.

Password protect your computer.

Put your devices "to sleep" or "hibernate" when leaving them. Do not leave them unlocked.

Shut down a device completely when traveling.

Be careful when connecting via USB, Bluetooth, etc.

Password protecting your phone, computer, and other devices
So you've picked an alphanumeric code. What kind of password should you make?
You should make a PASSPHRASE!(4+ words)
"Ok, for my computer password and phone password, that makes sense. But what about all my other passwords?"
Those should also be unique, but also very long to make it difficult for computers to guess them.
"That seems hard to remember and keep track of. You can't expect me to keep all of those passwords and remember them."
PASSWORD MANAGER!
Password Managers

+ Lots of options available, e.g. KeePass, 1Password, LastPass.

+ Encrypts your passwords.

+ Just requires one password (master password) to remember, which unlocks the others.

+ Great for saving passwords and security questions.

Encrypting your devices

Protects the data on your devices when they are lost, stolen, seized

iOS: on by default :)

Chromebook: on by default :)

Android: You can enable it under settings

Mac OS X: FileVault: You can enable it for your internal and external disks

Windows: BitLocker: You can enable it for your internal and external disks

Make backups of your disk encryption password!

Speaking of backups....
Data loss is a real risk.

Maintain local and/or remote backups.

Questions about locking devices, passwords, password managers and disk encryption?

Phishing

People can create websites that look legitimate and trick you into entering your password.
Avoid clicking on unknown links, and especially do not enter your password on the wrong site
How would you know if that was really a Google login page?
2 Factor Auth

Provides a second layer of protection to your online accounts

Real world example:
Something you have (key) plus something you know (combination)

Online example:
Something you have (your cell phone) plus something you know (password)

Available for many Google, Facebook, banks etc.

You can also ask other providers, e.g. cell phone company, to enforce a PIN number

Malware, Viruses, etc.

Avoid opening files without verifying in person or over the phone that someone really sent it to you.

To be safer, open a file in Google Drive rather than directly on your computer.

Apply security updates
Use anti-malware tools provided by your operating system (Windows Defender)
What are the risks of a phishing or malware attack?

Losing access to your accounts
